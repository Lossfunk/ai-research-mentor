This is ambitious for 9 months. The main risk is scope creep—trying to solve privacy, reliability, AND grid optimization simultaneously. Here's a realistic path:

**Months 1-3: Foundation & Baseline**
- Build a simplified grid simulator (don't over-engineer). Focus on a single use case like voltage control or demand response.
- Implement a non-federated RL baseline (PPO or DDPG). This is your benchmark—if you can't beat it, federated learning is pointless.
- **Reliability check**: Run 100+ seeds with different initial conditions. If variance is high, your problem is unstable, not your method.

**Months 4-6: Federated Core**
- Add federated averaging with differential privacy (DP-SGD). Start with loose privacy budgets (ε=10) to isolate performance degradation.
- **Critical test**: Compare federated vs. centralized performance gap. If it's >15%, your aggregation is broken, not your privacy.
- Implement Byzantine-robust aggregation (e.g., Krum or trimmed mean). Test with 20% malicious nodes injecting random updates.

**Months 7-9: Integration & Validation**
- Tighten privacy (ε=1-2) and test reliability under communication failures (drop 30% of nodes mid-training).
- **Make or break**: Run on real grid data if you have it. Simulators lie. If you don't have data, your "smart grid" claim is weak—scope down to "simulated microgrid."
- Write the paper focusing on the privacy-reliability tradeoff curve, not just "we built a system."

**Red flag**: If by month 4 your federated version isn't within 10% of centralized performance, pivot. The problem is likely your RL formulation, not the federation. Don't waste months tuning privacy parameters on a broken baseline.
