Remote AI work is absolutely possible, but you need to reverse the typical order: **demonstrate value first, then ask for access**.

**For projects:**
Skip the "volunteer" boards. Instead:
1. **Pick 2-3 active open-source ML projects** (Hugging Face, PyTorch, JAX repos) and make *substantial* contributions over 4-6 weeks. Fix real bugs, not typos. This becomes your proof-of-work.
2. **Document your learning** publicly. Write one technical deep-dive per week on a paper or implementation challenge. Quality > quantity. Tweet threads or Substack work fine.
3. **Target specific labs**: Find 5 researchers whose work you genuinely understand. Reference their papers in your writing, then email them with a *specific technical question* or insightâ€”not a request to join. If your point is sharp, they'll notice.

**For mentorship:**
Formal "mentorship circles" are mostly noise. Better:
- **Join a paid, cohort-based course** where instructors are actual practitioners (e.g., Full Stack Deep Learning, ML Collective). The network is the real value.
- **Start a reading group** with 3-4 peers at your level. Meet weekly, present papers. This forces clarity and creates accountability that mimics good mentorship.

**What doesn't work**: Cold LinkedIn messages, generic "I'm passionate about AI" emails, or applying to remote RA positions without a public portfolio.

Build the portfolio first. The opportunities will follow.
