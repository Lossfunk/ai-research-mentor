I understand the challenge of navigating research without built-in guidance. Here are concrete, structured pathways that create their own mentorship scaffolding:

### **1. Programs That *Are* the Mentor***
These replace traditional mentorship with structured curricula and cohorts:

- **MLH Fellowship** (fellowship.mlh.io): 12-week remote programs where you work on open-source AI projects with student teams and engineering mentors. Application deadlines are ~2 months before each session (Fall, Spring, Summer).
  
- **Google Summer of Code**: Not just coding—you write proposals, get paired with org mentors, and do sustained technical work. Many AI/ML orgs (TensorFlow, PyTorch, scikit-learn) participate. Applications open March.

- **REU Programs**: NSF funds many Research Experiences for Undergraduates. Search "NSF REU AI"—some are specifically for first-gen/minority students (e.g., Caltech's WAVE, MIT's MSRP). Deadlines are typically December-February for summer.

- **CRA-W/CDC**: Computing Research Association has *free* virtual mentoring workshops, grad school prep, and research cohorts specifically for underrepresented students. Their "Grad Cohort" and "URMD" programs are life-changing.

### **2. Self-Organizing Learning Communities**
These create peer mentorship networks:

- **Papers with Code**: Don't just read papers—click "Code" and *reimplement* one. Their "Trending" page shows what's actually reproducible. Join their Discord to ask authors questions directly.

- **EleutherAI Discord**: A community of 10,000+ people actually *doing* open-source AI research. They have reading groups, project channels, and welcome newcomers who put in effort. Lurk, then ask questions on their #beginners channel.

- **Local Reading Groups**: Create one. Post flyers in your CS department: "AI Paper Reading Group for First-Gen Students." Meet weekly, pick papers from *Transactions on Machine Learning Research* (free, open-access). Structure creates accountability.

### **3. The "Visible Apprenticeship" Strategy**
Since you lack a mentor's network, make your work findable:

- **GitHub as a Lab Notebook**: Don't just post code. Write detailed READMEs explaining *why* you chose a model, what failed, and questions you have. Tag relevant repos. Researchers *do* discover contributors this way.

- **Blog Your Learning Curve**: Use Substack or Medium to write "I tried to implement X and here's what I learned" posts. Tag authors on Twitter when you write about their work. Many will respond with guidance.

- **Replicate, Don't Just Reproduce**: Take a paper, re-run it, then *extend* it slightly (new dataset, ablation study). Publish your results on arXiv's *ML Reproducibility Challenge* or as a GitHub repo. This is a legitimate research contribution.

### **4. Time-Boxed Research "Sprints"**
Create structure where none exists:

- **30-Day Challenge**: Pick one problem (e.g., "fine-tune LLaMA on my university's course catalog"). Spend 1 hour/day. End with a 2-page report and a demo. This builds a portfolio *fast*.

- **Kaggle Competitions**: The discussion forums are goldmines of working researchers sharing code. Don't aim to win—aim to understand the top solutions' notebooks.

### **5. Institutional Resources You're Not Using**
First-gen students often miss these:

- **Your university's Office of Undergraduate Research**: They *exist* to help you find paid research positions and write proposals. They often have special funding for first-gen students.

- **Professors' *office hours***: Not for homework. Bring a paper you read and one specific question. "I'm trying to understand why Theorem 2 assumes convexity—could you walk me through the intuition?" This is how you convert teaching into mentorship.

- **McNair Scholars Program**: If your school has it, this is *literally* designed for first-gen, low-income PhD prep. Includes stipends, GRE prep, and research funding.

The key is to **replace the mentor's role with structured communities and public accountability**. Your disadvantage becomes an advantage: you'll document everything clearly because you *have* to, which makes you a better researcher.
