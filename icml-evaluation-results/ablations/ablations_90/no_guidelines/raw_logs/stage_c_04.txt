"Zero compute" is a real constraint, but it's not a barrier to meaningful research. Many foundational breakthroughs—from Shannon's information theory to Einstein's relativity—were made with little more than pen and paper. Here's a practical guide:

### **Clarify Your Constraint**
"Zero compute" usually means one of:
- Only a basic laptop (no GPU, limited RAM)
- No cloud budget
- No access to clusters
- Need to work offline

Even a 10-year-old laptop can handle text editing, PDFs, and simple scripting. That's enough.

---

### **High-Impact, Low-Compute Research Strategies**

**1. Theoretical & Conceptual Work**
- **Mathematical modeling**: Develop formal frameworks, prove theorems, derive bounds. Complexity theory, algorithm design, information theory, and cryptography are full of open problems needing conceptual breakthroughs, not cycles.
- **Paradoxes & thought experiments**: Identify contradictions in existing literature. A sharp counterexample can overturn years of empirical work.
- **Taxonomy & formalization**: Create precise definitions that clarify fuzzy concepts. Many fields (AI safety, interpretability, fairness) suffer from sloppy terminology.

**2. Deep Literature Synthesis**
- **Meta-analysis**: Statistically combine published results to find patterns the original authors missed. Tools like *metafor* in R run on any machine.
- **Error archaeology**: Re-examine published experiments for statistical flaws, unstated assumptions, or reproducibility issues. This needs careful reading, not compute.
- **Connecting silos**: Translate insights between disconnected fields. Breakthroughs often come from import/export of ideas (e.g., physics → machine learning).

**3. Human-Powered Research**
- **Expert interviews**: Study how practitioners make decisions. Record, transcribe (manually or with free tiers), and perform qualitative analysis.
- **Case studies**: Deep dive into 5-10 critical examples. Rich, contextual understanding beats big data for many questions.
- **Ethnography**: Observe how teams build systems. The bottlenecks are often social, not technical.

**4. Dataset & Benchmark Creation**
- **Curate small, high-quality datasets**: Manually annotate 500 examples with rich metadata. A meticulously crafted dataset for a niche problem can be more valuable than a massive, noisy one.
- **Design evaluation protocols**: Create rubrics that expose failures in current systems. The "winograd schema" challenge didn't need compute—just clever design.

**5. Algorithmic Thinking Without Implementation**
- **Design & analyze**: Write pseudocode, prove correctness, derive complexity. Publish the algorithm; others will implement it if it's valuable.
- **Lower bounds**: Prove what *cannot* be done efficiently. These results age better than empirical claims.

---

### **Field-Specific Examples**

- **AI/ML**: Study inductive biases, prove generalization bounds, analyze failure modes in published models (using their outputs, not training), design prompting strategies.
- **Systems**: Formalize consistency models, prove correctness of distributed protocols, analyze trade-offs theoretically.
- **HCI**: Conduct user studies with paper prototypes, analyze interaction logs from public sources.
- **Biology**: Develop theoretical models of evolution, analyze sequence patterns by eye (classical genetics did this), write review papers on overlooked mechanisms.
- **Physics**: Analytical solutions to toy models, dimensional analysis, symmetry arguments.

---

### **Making It Impactful**

1. **Target the "why"**: Computational research often answers "what works." Low-compute research excels at "why it works" or "why it fails."
2. **Be ruthlessly precise**: Without experimental flash, your logic must be airtight. Use formal notation, spell out assumptions, anticipate objections.
3. **Collaborate asymmetrically**: Partner with compute-rich labs. You provide the theory, problem framing, or analysis; they run experiments. Many PIs are desperate for deep thinkers who can guide experiments meaningfully.
4. **Publish in right venues**: Theory conferences (FOCS, STOC), survey journals (*ACM Computing Surveys*), or workshops on *understanding* rather than *scaling*.

---

### **Zero-Cost Compute "Hacks"**

If you need *some* compute:
- **Google Colab free tier**: 12 hours of GPU/day (T4). Enough for small experiments.
- **Kaggle**: 30 hours/week of GPU, plus free datasets.
- **University machines**: SSH into department servers (often underutilized).
- **Borrow time**: Ask authors of papers to share their trained models for analysis.

---

### **Mindset Shift**

The current culture overvalues compute-intensive research because it's *legible*—you can see the experiments running. But legibility ≠ importance. Many of the hardest problems (value alignment, scientific paradigms, theoretical foundations) are bottlenecked by *thinking*, not *compute*.

Your constraint is a filter: it forces you to work on problems where insight, not infrastructure, is the limiting factor. Those are often the most important ones.

**Start today**: Pick a paper you disagree with. Spend a week trying to construct a counterexample or find its hidden assumptions. That's research.
