**Short answer**: It's a steep climb but not impossibleâ€”think 6-12 months of dedicated learning rather than a few weekends. Here's a realistic breakdown:

---

### **What Makes This Hard**

Graph neural networks (GNNs) sit at the intersection of:
- **Advanced ML concepts** (graph theory, deep learning)
- **Complex data handling** (satellite imagery, NetCDF files, geospatial coordinates)
- **Significant coding** (Python, PyTorch/TensorFlow, specialized libraries)

Without coding experience, you're essentially learning 3-4 distinct skill sets simultaneously.

---

### **Prerequisites You'll Need**

| Skill | Why It Matters | Time Estimate |
|-------|----------------|---------------|
| **Python basics** | File handling, loops, functions | 1-2 months |
| **Data science libs** | NumPy (arrays), Pandas (tables), Xarray (climate data) | 1 month |
| **PyTorch fundamentals** | Tensors, autograd, training loops | 2-3 months |
| **Graph theory basics** | Nodes, edges, adjacency matrices | 2-3 weeks |
| **PyTorch Geometric** | GNN implementations | 1 month |

**Total: ~6 months of consistent practice** before you can meaningfully work on your project.

---

### **Step-by-Step Learning Path**

**Phase 1: Python & Data (Months 1-3)**
1. **Learn Python**: *Python Crash Course* (book) or CS50's Introduction to Programming (free)
2. **Practice with climate data**: 
   - Install Xarray: `pip install xarray`
   - Load NSIDC sea ice concentration data: `xr.open_dataset('seaice_conc_daily.nc')`
   - Try simple tasks: calculate monthly averages, plot trends with Matplotlib

**Phase 2: Deep Learning (Months 4-6)**
1. **PyTorch basics**: Fast.ai's *Practical Deep Learning for Coders* (free, hands-on)
2. **Run simple vision models**: Classify sea ice vs. open water using CNNs (easier than GNNs!)
3. **Understand graphs**: Take the *Graph Representation Learning* course on Stanford's YouTube

**Phase 3: Graph Nets (Months 7-9)**
1. **PyTorch Geometric tutorials**: Run their 10 introductory notebooks line-by-line
2. **Simplify your problem**: 
   - **Easy start**: Represent each grid cell in a sea ice map as a graph node
   - **Use pre-built models**: `torch_geometric.nn.GCNConv` instead of coding from scratch
3. **Replicate a paper**: Find a GNN+sea ice paper on arXiv and try to reproduce a small part

---

### **Feasible Alternatives to Start NOW**

If 6+ months feels daunting, consider these **coding-optional** entry points:

1. **Point-and-click GNNs**: 
   - **Google Earth Engine** (JavaScript/Python API but has GUI)
   - **No-code ML platforms** (Azure ML, Google AutoML) *don't support GNNs yet*

2. **Start with simpler ML**:
   - **Random Forests** for sea ice classification using scikit-learn (much easier than GNNs)
   - **Example**: Use latitude, longitude, temperature, past ice concentration as features

3. **Collaborate**: 
   - Find a CS student/researcher who needs a domain expert (you provide sea ice knowledge, they code)
   - Join climate ML communities: [Climate Change AI](https://climatechange.ai/), [Pangeo](https://pangeo.io/)

---

### **Sea Ice-Specific Resources**

- **Data**: NSIDC (free sea ice concentration, thickness), ECMWF (reanalysis)
- **Python libs**: 
  - `xarray` + `rioxarray` (handle NetCDF/GeoTIFF)
  - `icepack` (specifically for sea ice modeling)
- **Example GNN paper**: *"Graph Neural Networks for Sea Ice Prediction"* (search arXiv:2205.03284)

---

### **Bottom Line**

**Feasible?** Yes, if you're willing to treat it like a part-time degree.

**Recommended?** Start with traditional ML (Random Forest) or CNNs on sea ice images first. GNNs are a 3rd or 4th project, not a 1st.

**Quick win**: Spend 2 weeks learning Python+Xarray and make a simple sea ice trend map. That momentum will tell you if you're ready for the long haul.
