Great transition! Your physics background is a huge asset—strong math, modeling intuition, and computational skills put you ahead of the curve. Here's a structured roadmap:

### **Phase 0: Leverage Your Strengths (Week 1)**
First, recognize what you already have:
- **Math**: Linear algebra, calculus, probability/statistics (you're likely solid)
- **Programming**: If you've used Python (NumPy/SciPy) or Julia, you're set. If not, brush up quickly.
- **Mindset**: Physics trains you to build models from first principles—this is invaluable for understanding *why* ML works.

**Gap to fill**: The ML software ecosystem and specific jargon/concepts.

---

### **Phase 1: Core ML Foundations (1-3 months)**

**1. Start with a high-level course:**
- **Fast.ai's "Practical Deep Learning"** (free) – Gets you building things *immediately*, which builds intuition fast. Don't worry if it feels "too practical"; you'll connect it to theory later.
- **Alternative**: Andrew Ng's **Machine Learning** (Coursera) if you want more traditional, math-heavy grounding.

**2. Read the "physicist's bridge" paper:**
- *"A high-bias, low-variance introduction to Machine Learning for physicists"* by Mehta et al. (2019). This maps ML concepts directly onto physics language.

**3. Learn PyTorch (not just TensorFlow):**
- It's more intuitive for research, and the physics/ML community heavily uses it.
- **Resource**: Official PyTorch tutorials + **"Deep Learning with PyTorch"** (free book).

**4. Key concepts to master:**
- Loss functions as energy minimization
- Gradient descent as dynamics
- Overfitting/regularization as bias-variance tradeoff (familiar from statistical physics)
- Bayesian thinking (you've likely done this)

---

### **Phase 2: Build a Physics-AI Project (Months 3-6)**

This is **critical** for your CV. Don't just do generic Kaggle competitions—build something that screams "physics PhD":

**Project Ideas:**
- **Physics-Informed Neural Networks (PINNs)**: Solve PDEs (e.g., Navier-Stokes, Schrödinger eq.) with neural nets. Use libraries like **DeepXDE** or **NeuralPDE.jl**.
- **ML for phase transitions**: Classify phases from Monte Carlo data (Ising model, etc.).
- **Surrogate modeling**: Replace expensive simulations with neural nets.
- **Quantum ML**: If you're from quantum physics, explore **PennyLane** or **TensorFlow Quantum**.

**Why this matters**: It shows you can apply ML to *hard* scientific problems, which is a niche where you have unique value.

---

### **Phase 3: Deepen Theory & Find Your Niche (Months 6-12)**

**Choose a specialization track:**

**Track A: Scientific ML (best fit)**
- Read *"Machine Learning and the Physical Sciences"* (Carleo et al., Rev. Mod. Phys. 2019)
- Study: Neural ODEs, equivariant neural networks, operator learning
- Follow: **NeurIPS/ICML workshops** on "ML for Physical Sciences"

**Track B: Core AI Theory**
- Dive into optimization theory, generalization bounds, neural network dynamics
- Read: *"Deep Learning"* (Goodfellow et al.) – Chapters 5-8
- Study: Bayesian deep learning, information theory approaches

**Track C: Applied AI (industry)**
- Focus on MLOps, large-scale systems
- Build: End-to-end pipelines, deployment projects

---

### **Phase 4: Portfolio & Transition (Ongoing)**

**GitHub is your new CV:**
- 2-3 polished projects with READMEs, visualizations, and clear physics motivation
- Contribute to open-source scientific ML packages (e.g., **JAX**, **Pyro**, **DeepXDE**)

**Networking:**
- Join **Physics ∩ ML** reading groups (many on Slack/Discord)
- Attend **APS March Meeting** ML sessions
- Follow key people on Twitter: @shoyer (JAX), @karpatne (PINNs), @mrocklin (Dask)

**Publications:**
- If aiming for research roles, co-author a paper in **Physical Review Letters** or **PNAS** applying ML to physics. This is often easier than a pure ML conference paper and highly valued.

---

### **Timeline & Job Targets**

- **3 months**: Ready for ML engineer roles in scientific computing (e.g., climate modeling, fusion)
- **6-12 months**: Competitive for AI research scientist roles at national labs (LANL, Sandia) or AI-for-science startups (DeepMind, Anthropic, SandboxAQ)
- **12+ months**: Can target pure AI research if you publish in top venues (NeurIPS/ICML)

**Key tip**: In interviews, frame your physics background as *feature*, not baggage. Talk about Hamiltonians when discussing loss landscapes, and phase transitions when discussing generalization.

---
