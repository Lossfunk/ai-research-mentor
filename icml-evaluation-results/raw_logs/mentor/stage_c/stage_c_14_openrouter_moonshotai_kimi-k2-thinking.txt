**Blunt assessment:**  
Federated RL for smart grids is **high-risk but publishable** only if you (1) aggressively ablate assumptions, (2) treat **grid reliability as a non‑negotiable constraint**, and (3) **attack your own privacy claims**. Many papers fail because they show learning curves but never prove safety or privacy under realistic failures. This roadmap is built to prevent that.

---

## 1. Problem framing and goals

**Problem.**  
Smart grids increasingly rely on distributed control (demand response, voltage regulation, storage dispatch). Federated reinforcement learning (FRL) promises privacy-preserving coordination across grid nodes, but:
- convergence is fragile under non-stationarity,
- reliability constraints are often violated during learning,
- privacy claims are rarely validated against real attacks.

**Goal.**  
Produce a **systematic ablation-driven evaluation** of FRL methods for smart grid control that answers:
- *When does FRL actually help vs hurt grid reliability?*
- *Which components are necessary for stability?*
- *What privacy guarantees survive adversarial scrutiny?*

**Scope (locked).**  
One grid control task (e.g., demand response or voltage control), one simulator, one FRL family (e.g., FedAvg-style policy learning).

---

## 2. Experiments (ablation-centric)

### Experiment 1: Centralized vs decentralized vs federated RL (foundational)

**Hypothesis.**  
FRL only outperforms centralized or local RL under specific heterogeneity and communication regimes.

**Setup.**  
- Compare centralized RL, local independent RL, and FRL with identical policy classes.
- Fixed reward and constraint formulation.

**Baselines.**  
- Rule-based grid controller  
- Centralized RL (upper bound)

**Evaluation metrics.**  
Cumulative reward, constraint violations (voltage/frequency), convergence time.

**Expected outcomes.**  
FRL shows benefits only in limited regimes; otherwise adds instability.

---

### Experiment 2: Core FRL mechanism ablations

**Hypothesis.**  
Most FRL performance comes from a small subset of design choices.

**Setup.**  
Ablate one at a time:
- aggregation frequency  
- client participation rate  
- policy vs value-function sharing  
- synchronous vs asynchronous updates

**Baselines.**  
- Full FRL configuration  
- Randomized aggregation

**Evaluation metrics.**  
Learning stability, variance across seeds, worst-case node performance.

**Expected outcomes.**  
Identification of *necessary* vs *decorative* FRL components.

---

### Experiment 3: Reliability stress tests (non-negotiable)

**Hypothesis.**  
FRL policies that look good on average fail under rare but realistic grid events.

**Setup.**  
- Inject load spikes, renewable intermittency, communication delays.  
- Evaluate during both training and deployment phases.

**Baselines.**  
- Conservative rule-based controller  
- Centralized RL with safety constraints

**Evaluation metrics.**  
Violation frequency, recovery time, maximum deviation from safe bounds.

**Expected outcomes.**  
Many FRL variants fail reliability tests; a small subset survives.

---

### Experiment 4: Privacy attack ablations

**Hypothesis.**  
Vanilla FRL leaks sensitive local information through gradients or policies.

**Setup.**  
- Perform gradient inversion, membership inference, and property inference attacks.  
- Compare with and without DP noise, secure aggregation, or partial sharing.

**Baselines.**  
- No-privacy FRL  
- Local-only RL (privacy upper bound)

**Evaluation metrics.**  
Attack success rate, privacy–performance tradeoff curves.

**Expected outcomes.**  
Quantified privacy leakage; DP/secure aggregation impose real performance costs.

---

### Experiment 5: Joint privacy–reliability trade-off

**Hypothesis.**  
Some privacy mechanisms catastrophically degrade grid reliability.

**Setup.**  
- Combine best-performing FRL variants with privacy defenses.  
- Re-run reliability stress tests.

**Baselines.**  
- Best non-private FRL  
- Conservative controller

**Evaluation metrics.**  
Reliability under privacy constraints, Pareto frontier analysis.

**Expected outcomes.**  
Clear limits on “acceptable” privacy in safety-critical grids.

## 3. Timeline (9 months)

**Month 1:**  
Task selection, simulator setup, reliability constraints finalized.

**Month 2:**  
Centralized vs local vs FRL baselines (Experiment 1).

**Month 3:**  
Core FRL mechanism ablations (Experiment 2).

**Month 4:**  
Extended convergence and seed-variance analysis.

**Month 5:**  
Reliability stress testing (Experiment 3).

**Month 6:**  
Privacy attack implementation (Experiment 4).

**Month 7:**  
Privacy defense integration and re-evaluation.

**Month 8:**  
Joint privacy–reliability trade-off study (Experiment 5).

**Month 9:**  
Synthesis, negative results consolidation, paper writing.

**Hard kill criteria:**  
If no FRL variant beats the rule-based controller *without violating constraints*, the paper pivots to a **failure analysis** contribution.

## 4. Resources

**Compute.**  
- Moderate CPU/GPU; simulation dominates, not model size.

**Tools.**  
- Power grid simulators (e.g., open-source distribution grid simulators)  
- RL frameworks (PyTorch-based)  
- Secure aggregation / DP libraries

**Datasets.**  
- Synthetic or public grid load and generation profiles.

**Evidence note.**  
There is no standardized benchmark for FRL in smart grids with privacy attacks. This work’s value is the **ablation rigor**, not raw performance.

---

## 5. Risks and mitigations

| Risk | Impact | Mitigation |
|---|---|---|
| Unstable learning | Non-publishable | Embrace failure analysis |
| Fake privacy claims | Reviewer rejection | Explicit attack evaluations |
| Overfitting simulator | Weak relevance | Stress-test parameters |
| Scope creep | Miss deadlines | Lock task early |

---

## 6. Stretch ideas or follow-up directions

- Formal safety guarantees for FRL  
- Human-in-the-loop grid oversight  
- Regulatory implications of privacy–reliability trade-offs

---

**Bottom line:**  
If your results don’t make FRL look *uncomfortable*, you didn’t ablate hard enough.

**Optional next step:**  
Write a one-page **reliability charter** stating which grid violations are absolutely unacceptable—and fail any method that breaks it.
