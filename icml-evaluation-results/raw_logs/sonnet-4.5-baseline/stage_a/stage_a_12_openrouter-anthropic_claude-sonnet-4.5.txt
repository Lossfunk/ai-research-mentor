## üìö **Reading Research Papers (Strategies for Non-Native Speakers)**

### **Strategy 1: The "Figures First" Method** ‚≠ê HIGHLY EFFECTIVE

**Most papers tell their story through visuals:**

**Reading order:**
1. **Figures & Tables** (5 min) - Understand main results visually
2. **Abstract** (5 min) - Get the gist
3. **Introduction** (10 min) - Context and motivation
4. **Conclusion** (5 min) - Main takeaways
5. **Methodology** (optional, 20 min) - Technical details if needed

**Why this works:**
- Figures use less complex language
- You build context before tackling dense text
- Can understand 70% of paper without reading every word

---

### **Strategy 2: Use AI Translation & Simplification Tools**

**Modern AI makes this MUCH easier:**

**For Reading Papers:**

**1. ChatGPT/Claude** (Free tier works!)
- **Use case**: Paste abstract/section, ask for simplification
- **Prompt**: "Explain this abstract in simple English: [paste text]"
- **Prompt**: "What are the key findings in bullet points?"
- **Prompt**: "Explain this like I'm a beginner"
- **Advantage**: Conversational, can ask follow-ups

**2. DeepL Translator** ‚≠ê BEST TRANSLATOR
- **Website**: deepl.com (free tier)
- **Use case**: Translate papers to your native language
- **Why it's better**: More natural than Google Translate
- **Tip**: Translate section-by-section, not whole paper
- **Languages**: 30+ languages supported

**3. Scholarcy**
- **Website**: scholarcy.com (free trial)
- **Use case**: Auto-summarize research papers
- **Output**: Bullet points, key findings, definitions
- **Advantage**: Extracts main ideas automatically

**4. Elicit**
- **Website**: elicit.org (free)
- **Use case**: Ask questions, get paper summaries
- **Example**: "What are recent advances in federated learning?"
- **Advantage**: Finds and summarizes relevant papers

**5. Semantic Scholar**
- **Website**: semanticscholar.org (free)
- **Feature**: "TL;DR" (one-sentence summary) for many papers
- **Feature**: "Influential Citations" (what's most important)
- **Advantage**: Quick overview before deep reading

### **Strategy 3: Build Technical Vocabulary Systematically**

**AI has ~500 core terms** - learn these and you'll understand 80% of papers!

**Your Vocabulary Building Plan:**

**Week 1-2: Core ML Terms (50 words)**
- **Resource**: "Machine Learning Glossary" (Google - developers.google.com/machine-learning/glossary)
- **Method**: 5 terms/day with examples
- **Practice**: Use in sentences, find in papers

**Week 3-4: Your Subfield Terms (50 words)**
- **Example**: If studying computer vision: "convolution," "pooling," "attention," etc.
- **Resource**: Wikipedia + YouTube videos in your language
- **Method**: Create flashcards (Anki app - free)

**Week 5+: Learn by Reading**
- **Tool**: Notion/Google Doc with your personal glossary
- **Method**: Every unknown term ‚Üí look up ‚Üí add to glossary
- **Review**: Weekly review of new terms

**Common AI Terms Cheat Sheet** (Start here):
```
Neural Network, Deep Learning, Training, Inference
```

### **Strategy 3: Prepare Before Participating**

**When asking questions:**

**Use this template:**
```
**Context**: [What you're trying to do]
**Problem**: [Specific issue]
**What I tried**: [Your attempts]
**Code/Error**: [Paste code or error message]
**Question**: [Specific question]

(Apologies if my English isn't perfect - not my first language!)
```

**Why this works:**
- Clear structure (easy to follow)
- Specific (easier to answer)
- Shows effort (community appreciates this)
- Acknowledging language barrier = people are more patient

**Tools to help:**
- **Grammarly** (free) - Check grammar before posting
- **DeepL** - Write in your language, translate, then post
- **ChatGPT** - "Improve this question for clarity: [paste]"
