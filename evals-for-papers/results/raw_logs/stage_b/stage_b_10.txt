**Intuition**: Hospital shift notes contain rich temporal patterns of patient care that self-supervised learning could capture, but the high-stakes healthcare context amplifies both the potential impact and ethical risks compared to general domain text.

**Why this is principled**: Clinical SSL work shows promise for capturing meaningful patient state representations [P1][P3], but healthcare applications require stricter privacy controls and validation standards than typical NLP research due to potential downstream effects on patient care.

## Prior Work Analysis

There's substantial prior work on self-supervised learning with clinical text, particularly around:

1. **Clinical note representations**: Early work established that clinical notes contain predictive signals beyond structured data [P4], with recent advances in contrastive learning for clinical risk prediction [P3] and multi-modal integration [P2].

2. **Temporal clinical data**: Sequential SSL approaches have been successfully applied to clinical time series [P1][P9], showing that temporal patterns in patient data can be effectively learned without supervision.

3. **Hospital-specific applications**: Recent work focuses on hospital course summarization [P5] and patient subtype identification [P6], suggesting shift notes could be valuable for understanding care patterns.

## Key Ethical Risks

**Immediate concerns:**
- **Re-identification risk**: Shift notes often contain incidental details (room numbers, staff names, timing patterns) that could enable patient re-identification even after de-identification
- **Care quality implications**: Models trained on shift notes might learn and perpetuate biases in care documentation or delivery patterns
- **Staff privacy**: Notes contain assessments of colleague performance that weren't intended for research use

**Downstream risks:**
- **Decision support bias**: If used for clinical decision support, SSL-discovered patterns might reinforce existing care disparities
- **Documentation gaming**: Staff might alter documentation patterns knowing they're being analyzed

## Three Concrete Experiments

**Experiment 1: Temporal Care Pattern Discovery**
*Hypothesis*: SSL can discover meaningful temporal patterns in nursing shift handoffs that predict patient deterioration. *Setup*: Collect 6 months of de-identified shift notes from medical floors, apply sequential SSL with masked language modeling and next-shift prediction. *Evaluation*: Test if learned representations predict ICU transfers within 24 hours, comparing against structured data baselines. *Expected outcome*: AUROC improvement of 0.05-0.10 over structured data alone, with interpretable attention patterns highlighting key handoff content.

**Experiment 2: Cross-Hospital Transfer**
*Hypothesis*: SSL patterns learned from one hospital's shift notes transfer to predict outcomes at different hospitals. *Setup*: Pre-train on Hospital A shift notes, fine-tune on limited data from Hospitals B and C for mortality prediction. *Evaluation*: Compare transfer performance versus hospital-specific training, measuring calibration across sites. *Expected outcome*: 80-90% of fully-supervised performance with 50% less target hospital data, but significant calibration drift requiring hospital-specific adjustment.

**Experiment 3: Bias Detection in Care Documentation**
*Hypothesis*: SSL reveals systematic differences in how staff document care for different patient demographics. *Setup*: Train contrastive SSL on shift notes, analyze representation clusters by patient race, gender, and insurance status. *Evaluation*: Measure demographic parity in representation space and correlation with actual care outcomes. *Expected outcome*: Significant clustering by demographics (p<0.001) that correlates with outcome disparities, providing concrete evidence of documentation bias.
