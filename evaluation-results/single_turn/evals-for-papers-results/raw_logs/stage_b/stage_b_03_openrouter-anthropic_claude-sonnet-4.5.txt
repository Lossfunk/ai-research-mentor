### Why This Isn't Novel or Feasible as Stated

1. **AGI (Artificial General Intelligence) doesn't exist yet**  
   - We have no consensus on how to build AGI, when it will arrive, or even a precise definition of what constitutes AGI
   - Current AI systems (including large language models) are narrow AI—they excel at specific tasks but lack general reasoning, transfer learning, and autonomous goal-setting at human levels
   - "Creating AGI" is not a research project; it's a long-term, multi-decade grand challenge involving the entire AI research community

2. **"Solving world hunger" is not a technical problem alone**  
   - World hunger is primarily a problem of **distribution, politics, economics, conflict, and infrastructure**—not lack of food production or knowledge
   - The UN FAO estimates we already produce enough food to feed 10 billion people; the challenge is access, equity, and systems
   - AI tools can help optimize supply chains, predict crop yields, or improve agricultural practices, but they cannot solve political instability, poverty, or governance failures

3. **The framing is too broad and vague**  
   - Research requires **falsifiable hypotheses** and **measurable outcomes**
   - "Create AGI" and "solve world hunger" are aspirational goals, not research plans
   - You need to identify a specific, tractable sub-problem where AI can make a measurable contribution

## How to Reframe This Into Real Research

**Example research question**: "Can reinforcement learning optimize last-mile food distribution in humanitarian crises to reduce delivery time and spoilage by 20%?"

### **Option 3: AI for Early Warning Systems**

## Concrete Experiments You Should Run (If You Reframe)

Assuming you pick a **specific, tractable problem** like crop yield prediction or supply chain optimization, here are three experiments:

### **Experiment 1: Baseline Comparison**
**Hypothesis**: Your AI method outperforms existing operational systems or simple baselines.

**Protocol**:
- Identify the current state-of-the-art (e.g., FAO's crop yield models, existing logistics software)
- Implement your method and at least two strong baselines (e.g., linear regression, random forest, a recent deep learning model)
- Evaluate on a held-out test set with real-world data
- **Metrics**: Prediction accuracy (RMSE, MAE), precision/recall for classification tasks, or operational metrics (cost savings, time reduction)

**Success criterion**: Your method should improve over the best baseline by a statistically significant margin (e.g., ≥10% error reduction).

---

### **Experiment 2: Generalization Across Regions or Seasons**
**Hypothesis**: Your model generalizes to new geographic regions or time periods.

**Protocol**:
- Train on data from one region/time period (e.g., East Africa 2015–2020)
- Test on a different region or future time period (e.g., South Asia 2021–2023)
- Compare to models trained on local data
- **Metrics**: Transfer learning performance, domain adaptation success

**Success criterion**: Your model should retain ≥70% of its in-domain performance when transferred.

---

### **Experiment 3: Actionability and Impact Simulation**
**Hypothesis**: Your AI system's predictions lead to measurable improvements in decision-making.

**Protocol**:
- Simulate decision-making scenarios (e.g., "If we had predicted this drought 3 months early, how much food aid could we have pre-positioned?")
- Use historical data to backtest your model's recommendations
- Quantify potential impact (e.g., tons of food saved, people reached, cost savings)
- **Metrics**: Counterfactual impact estimates, cost-benefit analysis

**Success criterion**: Demonstrate that acting on your model's predictions would have improved outcomes by ≥15% in historical scenarios.

## Bottom Line

**"Creating AGI to solve world hunger" is not a research project—it's a science fiction premise.** If you want to do meaningful work at the intersection of AI and food security:

1. **Pick a specific, measurable sub-problem** (e.g., crop yield forecasting, supply chain optimization, early warning systems)
2. **Identify existing methods and data** (what's already being done?)
3. **Propose a concrete AI technique** (e.g., graph nets, transformers, RL) and explain why it's suited to the problem
4. **Design falsifiable experiments** with clear baselines, metrics, and success criteria
5. **Engage with domain experts** (agronomists, economists, NGOs) to ensure your work is grounded in real-world needs

Research is about solving **specific, tractable problems** with **measurable impact**—not grand, vague aspirations.
